[13:01] Siddharth Mathapati:
Thanks for making the time. Today I want to close on three things. One,
the recommended experience mix and pyramid. Two, the primary location and
whether we're a single-city or a hub-and-spoke model. Three, the
recommended org structure and who reports to whom. Anjali will walk us
through the benchmark data first — Anjali, over to you.

[13:02] Anjali Krishnan:
Thanks Siddharth. Quick reminder on methodology. We benchmarked 47 GCCs in
our dataset across the three shortlisted cities, filtered to retail, CPG,
and adjacent verticals — so companies whose talent needs look similar to
M&V's. On top of that, we ran a Naukri and LinkedIn active-candidate
scrape in August, deduplicated it, and cross-referenced with our
compensation panel. Six functions, three cities, three seniority bands.
That's what feeds the numbers in the file we shared this morning.

Two headlines. First, the Bengaluru premium over Hyderabad has compressed
sharply — for senior data engineering it's down from 28 percent to 14
percent in two years. Second, Pune's Finance Ops market is meaningfully
better than either Bengaluru or Pune on both cost and stability — and
this is the finding that pushes us toward a hub-and-spoke recommendation
rather than a pure single-city build.

[13:05] Aditya Rangan:
Anjali, on the Bengaluru versus Hyderabad question specifically — my
worry is quality, not cost. If I hire a senior data engineer in
Hyderabad, am I actually getting the same person I'd get in Bengaluru?

[13:06] Anjali Krishnan:
Honest answer — at the senior end, no, not quite. Our skill index for
Data and ML shows Bengaluru at 100, Hyderabad at 91. For Cloud and
Platform Engineering the gap is smaller — Bengaluru 100, Hyderabad 96.
Where I would place a bet on Hyderabad without hesitation is
mid-band Tech — SDE II, Data Engineer with 4 to 8 years, Cloud engineers.
For senior Data Science, over 10 years, Bengaluru is still the answer.

[13:07] Aditya Rangan:
That matches what I would have guessed. So the question isn't Bengaluru
versus Hyderabad — it's whether we can afford Bengaluru.

[13:08] Siddharth Mathapati:
Correct. And based on the cost model in the second tab of the file, we
can. A Bengaluru-primary build lands blended cost at about 41 percent
below the UK baseline in Year 3, versus about 44 percent for Hyderabad
and about 43 percent for Pune. The Bengaluru delta on cost is smaller
than most people assume, and the delta on capability is larger. So on a
risk-adjusted basis, Bengaluru is the right primary hub.

[13:09] Fiona McBride:
I want to pull on that thread. On Data and Analytics — what does the
senior end of the market actually look like? I need principal-grade data
scientists who can partner with my UK team as peers, not as executors.

[13:10] Anjali Krishnan:
For principal-grade — 12 plus years with real ownership experience — the
Bengaluru pool is roughly three to four times deeper than Hyderabad and
roughly six times deeper than Pune. This is the number that matters most
for your specific ask, Fiona. You will not build the analytics
capability you're describing in Hyderabad in Year 1.

[13:11] Fiona McBride:
That settles it for me on Data.

[13:12] David Okafor:
Can I ask about attrition? We've heard scary numbers about Bengaluru.

[13:12] Anjali Krishnan:
Bengaluru Tech attrition in our 2026 dataset averages 15.8 percent —
lower than Hyderabad at 17.2 and much lower than Gurugram at 22.6. The
"Bengaluru is worse on attrition" perception is about three to four
years out of date. Where Bengaluru is worse is on senior engineering
compensation inflation — 9 to 11 percent year on year versus about 7 to
8 in Hyderabad. So you pay more to retain, but you do retain.

[13:14] David Okafor:
And Pune?

[13:14] Anjali Krishnan:
Pune is the lowest attrition of the three across the board. Tech 13.4,
Finance Ops 11.1 percent. Which is unusually low. This is one of the two
main reasons we recommend Pune for Finance Ops rather than keeping
Finance Ops in Bengaluru.

[13:15] David Okafor:
What's the other reason?

[13:15] Anjali Krishnan:
The talent pool. Pune's active-candidate pool for Finance Ops is about
36,500. Bengaluru is 28,000, Hyderabad 24,000. And more importantly, the
Pune pool is disproportionately alumni of the large captives — Genpact,
WNS, Accenture — which means they arrive already trained on the
processes and controls that M&V's Finance Ops actually cares about. You
get faster ramp and lower induction cost.

[13:17] Siddharth Mathapati:
David, this is the crux of the hub-and-spoke recommendation. If we put
Finance Ops in Bengaluru, we're paying about 12 to 15 percent more for
the same seat, we're absorbing higher attrition, and we're recruiting
from a shallower pool. Splitting it out to Pune from Year 2 gives us
better economics, better stability, and better ramp — with the trade-off
that we manage a second city.

[13:18] Callum Radcliffe (joined):
Sorry — joined late. Are we landing on Bengaluru plus Pune?

[13:19] Siddharth Mathapati:
That's our recommendation, and the room is broadly aligned. Bengaluru is
the primary hub from Day 1 — Tech, Data, Cloud and Security,
Customer Support, Leadership. Pune stands up in Year 2 for Finance Ops
and a small HR Ops CoE. Steady-state — around 560 in Bengaluru, around
160 in Pune.

[13:20] Callum Radcliffe:
Why not Pune in Year 1 as well?

[13:20] Siddharth Mathapati:
Two reasons. One, execution focus — running one city well in Year 1 is
hard enough for a first-time GCC; running two doubles the operational
load on a Site Leader who's still building the team. Two, Finance Ops is
not the tower you want in the first ninety days. You want Tech and Data
in first, delivering visible wins, so the internal narrative in the UK
lands as "capability we couldn't build here" — which is what David
asked for at kickoff. Finance Ops in Month One reads too much like a
back office and it colors everything after it.

[13:22] David Okafor:
That framing helps a lot.

[13:22] Callum Radcliffe:
Alright. Let's talk org structure. This is the piece I've been going back
and forth on.

[13:23] Siddharth Mathapati:
We recommend a matrix. Site Leader at Managing Director grade, external
hire, reporting solid-line to Callum. Each functional tower head —
Tech, Data, Finance Ops, HR Ops, CS — reports solid-line to their
counterpart in the UK, dotted-line to the Site Leader. Underneath, we
use pod-based delivery — pods of 8 to 12 aligned to UK business units.
And we overlay three Centers of Excellence — Data and AI, Cloud
Platform, and Cybersecurity — funded centrally, drawing from all
towers.

[13:25] Aditya Rangan:
I want to push back on the reporting lines. If my head of Tech in
Bengaluru reports solid-line to me and dotted-line to the Site Leader,
the Site Leader has no real authority. Isn't that the failure mode
where the GCC ends up as five separate silos?

[13:26] Siddharth Mathapati:
It's a good challenge. It IS a failure mode when the Site Leader is
weak. If you invert the reporting — solid-line to the Site Leader,
dotted-line to global function heads — you get a different failure
mode, which is that global functions underinvest in the GCC because
they don't own it. In our dataset, of about eighty-plus first-time
GCCs, the version that works best is solid-line to global function
heads, dotted-line to Site Leader — BUT with two things attached. One,
the Site Leader has explicit authority over people, culture, and site
economics — so hiring, promotions above a certain level, and comp
decisions require Site Leader sign-off. Two, the Site Leader sits on
the global function heads' leadership team as a full member, not an
observer.

[13:29] Aditya Rangan:
That's more nuanced than I've heard it described before. I can accept
that.

[13:29] David Okafor:
On the Site Leader — how senior are we talking?

[13:30] Siddharth Mathapati:
Managing Director grade. Fifteen plus years, at least seven of which
running a GCC or a captive in India. Probably an ex-MNC leader —
someone who's built and scaled at least one center from 100 to 500 plus.
Fully-loaded package around 2.2 to 2.8 crores. Non-negotiable.

[13:31] David Okafor:
That's a big number.

[13:32] Siddharth Mathapati:
It's the single highest-leverage line in the whole cost model. Every GCC
we've seen fail in Year 2 or 3, without exception, under-invested at
this hire. Every one that scaled to a real capability center over-invested.

[13:33] David Okafor:
Understood. I'll defend it.

[13:33] Callum Radcliffe:
On the experience pyramid — where did we land?

[13:34] Anjali Krishnan:
Recommendation is 45 percent at 0 to 3 years, 35 percent at 4 to 8, 15
percent at 9 to 14, 5 percent at 15 plus. This is materially more senior
than the original 65/30/5 draft, and it's the shape you need for the
capability-center ambition. Cost impact is about 8 percent higher year-1
comp, but roughly 22 percent lower rework rate from UK teams — which
more than pays for it by end of Year 2.

[13:36] Callum Radcliffe:
Fine. That's approved subject to seeing the cost impact in the model.

[13:36] Siddharth Mathapati:
Two more things I want to close before we're done. First, back-office
ratios. We're modeling one HR Ops person per 25 core FTE, one Finance
Ops per 40, one IT support per 75, one facilities per 100, and one
leadership plus PMO per 60. These are steady-state — in the ramp-up
year we run about 40 percent hotter on each to build the plumbing
before the core teams arrive.

[13:37] David Okafor:
Are those ratios normal? One per 25 for HR feels heavy.

[13:38] Anjali Krishnan:
It's what we see in well-run mid-size GCCs. Below one per 30 in HR you
start dropping candidate experience, and in a hot Bengaluru market that
directly hits your offer-to-join ratio. Below one per 45 in Finance you
start seeing controls slippage. These are load-tested numbers, not
theoretical.

[13:39] David Okafor:
Fine.

Second and last — risks. Top three we want the Board to see. Site

   Leadership+PMO 1:60. Ramp-up year at ~1.4x these.
